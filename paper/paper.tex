\begin{abstract}
	\textit{Background:} Children in early autism intervention settings vary widely in age, engagement, and responsiveness to instruction. Practitioners often use short pre-session preparation activities, but the best preparation for a given child is uncertain.
	
	\textit{Objective:} To estimate the effect of different pre-session preparation activities on immediate engagement and readiness for learning, using a randomized repeated-measures crossover design.
	
	\textit{Methods:} This study was conducted at Maya Care and Grow, an early autism intervention center in Agartala, India. Approximately 25 children aged 3–14 years participated in a within-subject randomized crossover study. Each child received one preparation condition per session, randomized in blocked order, with no same condition repeated consecutively. Preparation conditions consisted of [Calming Prep / Sensory Prep / No Prep] or a comparable set of 3–4 existing preps plus control, depending on the final intervention structure. The primary outcome was practitioner-rated engagement during the learning session, assessed using a structured 1–5 scale. Secondary outcomes included [time to answer questions / prompts needed / task completion time]. Mixed-effects and/or Bayesian hierarchical models were used to estimate treatment effects at both the individual and group levels.
	
	\textit{Results:} [Insert sample characteristics, treatment balance, primary outcome estimates, secondary outcome estimates, and inter-rater reliability.]
	
	\textit{Conclusions:} A randomized repeated-measures design in a real-world autism intervention setting can support estimation of personalized responses to preparatory activities. Findings may help identify which preparation type is most suitable for individual children and may inform future personalized intervention work.
\end{abstract}

	\section{Introduction}
	
	
	Children receiving early autism intervention often differ substantially in learning level, developmental profile, behavioral state, and response to session structure. At Maya Care and Grow in Agartala, India, children typically attend 2–16 hours per week, with sessions lasting 1–2 hours, and no child attends more than one session per day. The center serves 25 children with a wide age range, from 3 to 14 years old.
	
	Before sessions begin, practitioners currently use short preparatory activities such as blocks, jumping and body massage, blowing bubbles, meditation, puzzles, clay, cutting paper, stringing beads, and music listening or singing. Some children receive no formal preparation if they appear naturally engaged. These choices are made based on practitioner judgment, child history, and current mood, but there is little confidence that the selected preparation is the one most likely to maximize learning engagement.
	
	This creates an opportunity for a randomized study in a setting where treatment delivery is already routine and where data collection is feasible through close practitioner involvement. Because the children differ greatly in response and session attendance is repeated over time, a personalized repeated-measures design is especially suitable.
