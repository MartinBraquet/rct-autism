\begin{abstract}
	Early autism intervention relies heavily on practitioner intuition to determine how best to prepare a child for a learning session, yet the heterogeneity of sensory and regulatory profiles in children with ASD means that no single preparation approach is likely to be universally optimal. This study examines whether a brief, structured pre-session preparation condition — Stimulating (vestibular and proprioceptive input), Calming (quiet fine-motor activity), or Child-Led (choice-board selection) — improves engagement during standardised learning blocks relative to no preparation, and whether the optimal condition differs meaningfully across children. Using a randomised N-of-1 crossover design in which each child serves as their own control, we collect session-level engagement scores (BRES-10, 1–10) from all eligible children enrolled at Maya Care and Grow, Agartala, over an eight-week data collection window of up to 32 sessions per child. Conditions are allocated via permuted-block randomisation at the session level, and a blinded rater scores a 20-minute standardised learning block following each preparation. A Bayesian hierarchical linear model — incorporating fixed effects for preparation condition, teacher identity, and age, alongside child-level random intercepts and random preparation slopes — is fitted to the accumulating data and evaluated weekly against pre-registered adaptive stopping rules: a Superiority rule (posterior probability > 0.90 that one condition is best for that child), an AIPE rule (two conditions are both effective and practically indistinguishable), and a ROPE rule (no preparation is meaningfully different from no preparation for that child). Expected outcomes include individual-level preparation recommendations for each child with quantified posterior confidence, group-level estimates of the average effect of each preparation type, and an empirical estimate of the value of personalisation over a one-size-fits-all approach — providing both an immediately actionable clinical tool for the centre and a methodological template for conducting rigorous adaptive trials in resource-constrained early intervention settings.
\end{abstract}
